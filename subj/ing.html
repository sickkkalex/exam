Malware & Cybersecurity

An exploration of digital threats and defenses. Learn how malicious software spreads across networks and what core measures you must take to protect systems, privacy, and sensitive data online.

What is Malware?

Malware is the general term used to describe malicious software designed to infiltrate computers and networks to steal data, encrypt files, or disable systems. The word is a blend of malicious and software, reflecting its harmful purpose.

Rather than acting in isolation, modern malware spreads rapidly by exploiting various channels:

  • Email Services: Malicious links and infected attachments sent directly to user inboxes.
  • Online Services: Compromised websites hosting automatic downloads or fake advertisements.
  • Peer-to-Peer Sharing: Shared files on torrent websites embedding hidden malicious payloads.
2

Common Types of Malware & Threats

Cybercriminals employ various software types, each tailored for a specific attack vector or criminal goal. The most common threats include:

Virus

A program that infiltrates a computer and causes it to malfunction, acting as though the system were "ill". It typically requires human action to propagate by attaching itself to clean files.

Spyware

A secret program that observes and reports on a user's online behavior without their knowledge, usually for advertising or data brokerage. It can also hijack display or pop-up settings.

Spam

Unwanted bulk emails sent to mass audiences, primarily for advertisement. While often just a nuisance, they can carry dangerous virus attachments or links to phishing pages.

Worm

An autonomous program that invades network computers and buries itself deep within the software. It replicates itself over the network to avoid deletion, clogging operations.

Trojan

A malicious program that disguises itself as an innocent or useful application. Once active inside a system, it opens a backdoor to let attackers take control and steal credit cards or passwords.

Botnet

A large group of infected computers controlled remotely by a criminal gang without the owners' knowledge. These "zombie" systems are coordinated to launch large cyber attacks.

Phishing

A fraudulent method of tricking users into revealing sensitive data like bank details, credentials, or pins. It takes the form of emails mimicking trusted institutions (e.g., banks or shipping companies).

3

How to Protect Yourself

To protect your computer and personal information, it is critical to implement defense strategies. Unit 15 outlines the four essentials of cybersecurity:

1. Install a Firewall

A firewall acts as a defensive wall around your computer (built into hardware or software). It checks incoming and outgoing traffic from the Internet and either blocks or allows data packages to pass based on configured security rules, preventing hackers from scanning open ports.

2. Keep Software Up to Date

Cybercriminals frequently exploit known security vulnerabilities in operating systems and third-party plugins (such as Java). Installing security updates offered by software providers (like Microsoft Windows Updates) patches these holes, neutralizing exploits.

3. Use Protection Software

Keep active security suites running:

  • Anti-virus software: Scans incoming emails, downloads, and files for viruses, worms, and Trojans, isolating them before damage occurs.
  • Anti-spyware programs: Alerts the user when spyware attempts to install, removing existing tracking programs.
Warning: Never install anti-virus software in response to a pop-up advertisement. There are dozens of fake anti-virus applications designed to simulate fake infection alerts on your screen, demanding credit card payments or installing actual ransomware.

4. Take Special Care with Email

Email is the most common infiltration point. Practice these precautions:

  • Attachments: Never open email attachments from unknown senders, as they are a primary source of infection.
  • Hyperlinks: Avoid clicking links blindly, especially inside messages asking you to confirm security data (phishing).
  • Filters: Set up aggressive spam filters. Never respond to a spam email—not even to click "unsubscribe"—as it confirms to the spammer that your address is active.
